Sunday morning — fight cardio session
1 session per week · ~55 min · your park · no equipment needed
Park tips — making it fight-specific
Use a phone interval timer app (Seconds, Interval Timer) set to your work/rest times. Chase the clock, not distance.
Your lap (~340m, ~2 min at tempo) is your round marker. Stop counting km — count effort intervals.
The twists and turns force lateral deceleration and re-acceleration — more sport-specific than a flat oval.
On sprint efforts, pick a landmark (bench, tree, corner) and go all-out to it. Distance doesn't matter — full effort for ~15–20 sec matters.
Rate each hard session out of 10. Aim for 8/10 on your hard rounds. Consistently 6/10 = too easy. 10/10 = went too hard.
Optional: shadow box during walk rest intervals. Keeps hands active and bridges running cardio to fight movement.

Target cut
Weight cut logic
How you drop 3–5 kg over 6 weeks
At 71kg with your training load you burn roughly 2400–2600 kcal/day to maintain weight. Eating ~2000 on training days and ~1700 on rest days creates a daily deficit of 400–900 kcal. Combined with 5 weekly sessions you should lose 0.5–0.75 kg per week — arriving at 66–68 kg by fight week without losing muscle. Do not go below 1600 kcal on any day. Below that you start burning muscle and your training quality collapses.
Protein target
Why 180–200g protein every day
In a caloric deficit with heavy training you need ~2.2–2.5g protein per kg bodyweight to preserve muscle. At 71kg that's 156–178g minimum — so 180–200g gives you a buffer. Your snacks alone cover ~80–100g if you hit cottage cheese, Greek yogurt, a tuna packet, and jerky daily. Meals need to deliver the other ~100g. Protein powder and creatine stack on top to make this easy to hit.
Training vs rest days
How to eat differently on each
Training days (Mon, Wed, Fri, Sat, Sun): ~2000 kcal. Include brown rice at lunch and optionally dinner on sparring days. Don't undereat — your MT quality tanks if you're running on empty.

Rest days (Tue, Thu): ~1700 kcal. Same protein, cut carbs — little or no brown rice. Fill volume with extra veg, eggs, and lean protein. Hunger on rest days is usually thirst — drink water first.
Daily — every single day
Protein powder
1–2 scoops per day (~25–50g protein). The most important use case for you is post-training on late nights — when you get home at 10pm from an 8pm session and don't want to cook, a shake takes 30 seconds and gets 25g protein into your muscles immediately. Also useful as a breakfast add-in or mid-afternoon snack when you're short on time. Don't use it to replace real meals — use it to fill gaps.
Daily — every single day
Creatine monohydrate
3–5g per day, taken at any consistent time — timing doesn't matter much, consistency does. Creatine's benefits are cumulative: it takes 2–3 weeks of daily use to saturate your muscles. It supports explosive power output, helps you recover faster between hard efforts (like rounds), and has zero downsides at this dosage. Take it with your protein shake or stir it into water. Don't cycle it — just take it every day throughout camp and beyond.
Training sessions only
Electrolyte drink
You already have this — good. Use it during and after every MT session and cardio session. When you're cutting weight and training hard you lose significant sodium, potassium, and magnesium through sweat. Plain water doesn't replace these. Symptoms of electrolyte depletion — cramping, fatigue, brain fog, poor sleep — are easily mistaken for overtraining. Zero-cal electrolytes are ideal since you're cutting.
Optional but useful
Magnesium glycinate — before bed
200–400mg before sleep. Magnesium is depleted rapidly by heavy training and is directly linked to sleep quality and muscle recovery — two things you've said are critical for you. Glycinate form is the gentlest on the stomach. This is inexpensive, widely available, and one of the most evidence-backed supplements for athletes in hard training. Worth considering if you notice poor sleep quality mid-camp.
Optional
Vitamin D3 + Omega-3
Both support recovery and reduce inflammation. If you're already getting sun exposure (Mexico City) you may not need D3. Omega-3 (fish oil, 1–2g EPA/DHA per day) helps with joint recovery and reduces soreness — useful if your body feels beat up in weeks 3–4. Neither are essential but both are low-cost and well-supported.
What NOT to take
Avoid pre-workouts and stimulant stacks
Pre-workout supplements with high caffeine, beta-alanine, or proprietary blends mask fatigue rather than addressing it — and they wreck your sleep when used before evening sessions. If you need extra energy for an 8pm session, a black coffee 45 min before is cleaner and won't interfere with sleep as badly as a pre-workout at that hour. Your sleep is your biggest recovery asset — protect it.

Recovery protocol
What you do between sessions is what determines how good your next session is
Sleep — your #1 recovery tool
Target 8–9 hours every night. Non-negotiable. No supplement or habit replaces this.
Keep the same sleep and wake times 7 days a week — even weekends. Consistency matters more than duration.
No screens 30–45 min before bed. After hard training your nervous system is fired up — it needs a wind-down signal.
Room temperature 17–19°C. Training raises your core temp — a cool room accelerates the drop your body needs for deep sleep.
Dark and quiet. Blackout curtains or an eye mask if needed.
400mg magnesium glycinate 1 hour before bed on hard training days — directly supports sleep depth and muscle recovery.
On 8pm training nights: eat your post-training snack immediately when you get home, then start winding down. Be in bed by midnight at the latest.
No caffeine after lunch. Coffee or tea at 3pm will still be in your system at 11pm.
Immediately after sparring (Wed + Sat)
Electrolytes first — before you even get changed. You've been sweating hard for 90 min.
Eat your post-training snack within 30 min of finishing. Don't skip because you're tired.
5–10 min of light stretching before bed — hips, neck, shoulders, lower back. Sparring compresses the spine and tightens hips. Two minutes per area is enough.
Finish your shower with 60 seconds of cold water. Reduces acute inflammation and shifts your nervous system from fight mode to rest mode faster.
No alcohol. Even one drink after sparring significantly impairs the overnight muscle repair process.
The day after sparring (Thu after Wed, Sun after Sat)
Thursday is a full rest day — no training. This is by design. Let the soreness clear.
A 20-min easy walk is ideal — not a run, just a walk. Keeps blood flowing and clears metabolic waste without adding stress.
Eat your full protein targets even though you're resting. Your muscles are actively repairing — they need fuel.
10 min of hip and lower body stretching in the afternoon — by the time Friday's MT session comes, you want to feel loose.
Sunday after Saturday sparring: cardio is scheduled — but keep Sunday's session honest, not brutal. You're on fatigued legs by design.
Tuesday + Thursday — how to actually rest
Rest day ≠ do nothing all day. Total inactivity slows recovery. Light movement (walking, stretching) speeds it up.
A 20-min walk after lunch is one of the highest-return habits in this whole plan. Aids digestion, activates your lymphatic system, clears waste from training.
Hit your protein targets — 180–200g. Don't under-eat on rest days thinking you're saving calories. Muscle repairs fastest when protein is available.
Cut carbs on rest days — no or minimal brown rice. Fill the volume with extra veg and protein instead.
Use rest days for social life, decompression, and sleep catch-up. They're a feature of the plan, not a weakness.
Don't add extra training on rest days because you "feel fine." Fatigue accumulates invisibly — you won't feel it until it peaks at the wrong time.
Warning signs — when to pull back
Weeks 3–4 are your peak load weeks. Some tiredness is normal and productive. These signs mean you've crossed into overreaching:
Soreness that doesn't clear between sessions — still sore on Friday from Wednesday sparring
Poor sleep despite being exhausted — you're tired but can't switch off
Declining performance in sparring — you feel slower, not sharper, week over week
Persistent low mood or irritability — especially if it's not like you
Loss of appetite or strong food aversion
What to do if you notice 2+ of the above
Drop one MT session that week — skip Friday or one sparring day, not both.
Skip the Sunday cardio session that week entirely.
Prioritise sleep above everything else for 2–3 days.
Eat at maintenance — don't cut calories during a recovery week.
One unplanned recovery week mid-camp will not hurt your fitness. Pushing through overreaching into the fight week will.
Daily habits — small things that compound
Walk after lunch (every day). 20 min. Improves digestion, reduces blood sugar spike, keeps lymphatic system active. Especially important on office days.
Hydration. Aim for 3L of water per day minimum on training days. Dehydration by even 2% impairs power output and decision-making in sparring.
Electrolytes during every training session. Not just water — you're sweating out sodium, potassium, magnesium. Cramping and brain fog mid-session are electrolyte issues, not fitness issues.
Creatine daily. Takes 2–3 weeks to fully saturate. Supports recovery between rounds and explosive output. Miss a day and take it the next — just stay consistent.
5 min of stretching before sleep. Hips, hip flexors, neck, shoulders. Every night. It takes less time than scrolling your phone and does more for next-day soreness than anything else.
Cold finish to showers on hard training days. 60 seconds cold at the end. Reduces inflammation, improves mood, helps nervous system shift to rest mode.
Protein within 30 min of finishing every training session. Even if it's just a shake. The window is real — don't miss it consistently.
